SOLAS

Mid-infrared spectroscopy + machine learning + AI

One scan. Multiple soil attributes. Faster decisions.

SOLAS is a soil analysis platform that uses MIR infrared spectroscopy combined with machine learning and AI to predict multiple physical and chemical soil attributes from a single scan. It is designed to reduce reliance on slow, labor-intensive wet chemistry and support greener, scalable soil testing.

The opportunity

Replacing slow wet chemistry with scalable soil sensing

Why the work started

Karen Daly leads a Teagasc group developing agri-tech innovation to improve the sustainability of the agri-food sector in Ireland, spanning soil health, water quality, sensing and spectroscopy. Commercial soil testing remains essential for environmental, quality and regulatory purposes, but many conventional analyses are slow, costly and dependent on labor-intensive wet chemistry.

What SOLAS does

Building on Teagasc expertise in soil science and spectroscopy, SOLAS uses mid-infrared spectral libraries and predictive models to estimate multiple soil parameters from a single scan. This creates a practical pathway to faster turnaround, lower analytical cost and lower chemical usage, without giving up the analytical value needed for monitoring and decision-making.

Why it matters now

The methodologies developed through SOLAS have the potential to support technology adoption at national scale, including future soil monitoring needs associated with the draft EU Soil Monitoring Law. Spectroscopy offers an efficient route to wider, more frequent and more affordable soil assessment.

Predicted attributes

What SOLAS can predict from MIR spectra

The current MIR workflow predicts the soil attributes below for mineral soils. These parameters matter because they shape nutrient supply, structure, carbon accounting, crop response, environmental risk and management planning.

Sand, silt and clay

Texture controls water retention, drainage, workability, nutrient holding capacity and soil classification.

Bulk density

Bulk density is essential for understanding compaction, rooting conditions and carbon stock calculations.

Organic matter

Organic matter supports soil structure, nutrient cycling, water holding and biological activity.

Total carbon and organic carbon

Carbon indicators are central for soil health, climate reporting and long-term land management.

Total nitrogen

Nitrogen status informs fertility management and helps explain crop productivity and nutrient balance.

pH in water and CaCl2

Soil pH influences nutrient availability, toxicity risk, liming strategy and overall agronomic suitability.

pH SMP

pH SMP helps estimate lime requirement and supports practical correction of soil acidity.

CEC (BaCl2)

Cation exchange capacity is a core measure of a soil's ability to retain and exchange nutrient cations.

Calcium and magnesium

These nutrients are important for structure, root function, plant nutrition and liming interpretation.

Iron

Iron is relevant to soil chemistry, redox behavior and nutrient interactions in mineral soils.

Chromium and cobalt

These trace elements can be important in environmental assessment and geochemical interpretation.

Integrated insight

Looking at these attributes together gives a fuller picture of soil function than single-test analysis alone.

How it works

From spectral signal to decision-ready soil information

01

Scan the soil

Samples are measured in the mid-infrared region to capture rich chemical and mineralogical information.

02

Pre-process the spectra

Spectral preprocessing aligns and cleans the MIR signal for robust comparison against calibration data.

03

Predict with ML and AI

Trained models estimate multiple soil attributes from the same scan while screening unrepresentative spectra.

04

Support action

Outputs can help guide soil monitoring, research, environmental assessment and smarter lab workflows.

Important note

Current applicability

At present, SOLAS models are designed for mineral soils. The current prediction scope is: organic matter below 30% (%OM < 30). Organic soils and peat-rich samples fall outside the intended operating range of the present models.
